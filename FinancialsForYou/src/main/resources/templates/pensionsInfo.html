What is a Pension?

A pension is a long-term savings plan designed to provide a steady income after retirement. In the United Kingdom, pensions serve as a vital component of financial security in later life, ensuring individuals can maintain their standard of living once they stop working. Pension contributions are invested over time, potentially growing through interest, dividends, and capital gains, resulting in a larger retirement fund.

  • Workplace Pension: A pension scheme arranged by your employer. Employers must auto-enrol eligible workers and contribute a minimum of 3% of qualifying earnings, with employees contributing at least 5%.
  • Defined Benefit (DB) Pension: Provides a guaranteed income based on your salary and years of service. Often referred to as 'final salary' or 'career average' schemes.
  • Defined Contribution (DC) Pension: Contributions are invested, and the pension pot size depends on investment performance. Common in modern workplace pensions.
  • Self-Invested Personal Pension (SIPP): A private pension giving individuals more control over where their pension money is invested.
